The Score Canvas: A New Approach to Musical Notation

Introduction

The Score Canvas is Kala’s composition interface—a radical departure from traditional musical notation. Where conventional notation evolved around the limitations of paper and the mechanics of acoustic instruments, Kala embraces what modern technology makes possible: capturing the full expressiveness of a musical gesture in a single drawing motion.

In traditional notation, a note is an instruction: “play this pitch, at this loudness, for this duration.” The performer interprets these instructions, adding expression through subtle variations in timing, dynamics, and pitch. In Kala, the composer captures these expressive nuances directly. When you draw a note with a pressure-sensitive pen, you’re not writing instructions—you’re performing the gesture itself, and that gesture becomes the composition.

The Frequency Staff: Color-Coded Scale Lines

A Staff That Follows the Music

Traditional sheet music uses a fixed staff—five lines representing specific pitches, with ledger lines extending above and below. This system works well for music based on the Western 12-tone scale, but it becomes awkward for other tuning systems and scales from around the world.

Kala takes a different approach. The staff lines are generated dynamically based on the active scale, with each line representing an actual pitch in that scale. The vertical position corresponds directly to frequency—higher pitches appear higher on the screen, lower pitches appear lower. The spacing between lines reflects the actual intervallic relationships in the scale, not an arbitrary grid.

The Color Code

Each scale degree has its own color, creating an immediate visual map of harmonic relationships:

Degree Color Musical Role
1 Red Tonic (home note)
2 Orange Second degree
3 Yellow Third degree
4 Green Fourth degree
5 Blue Fifth (dominant)
6 Indigo Sixth degree
7 Violet Seventh degree
8+ Pink, Cyan, Gray Extended degrees

The tonic (red) and fifth (blue) are drawn with thicker lines, emphasizing the foundational harmonic relationship that appears in virtually all musical traditions. This color system works regardless of which scale you choose—the red line is always “home,” whether you’re working in Western major, Arabic Maqam Rast, or Indonesian Pelog.

Logarithmic Frequency Space

The vertical axis uses logarithmic spacing, matching how humans perceive pitch. Each octave occupies the same vertical distance (100 pixels by default), so the visual distance between any two notes reflects their perceived intervallic distance. A perfect fifth looks the same size whether it’s in the bass register or the treble.

This means that when you look at the score, you’re seeing pitch relationships as you hear them—a major third in one octave looks identical to a major third in another, because they sound the same in terms of interval quality.

World Scales

Kala includes scales from musical traditions around the globe:

Western Tunings - Just Intonation (pure harmonic ratios) - Pythagorean (based on perfect fifths) - Equal Temperament (modern piano tuning) - Quarter-Comma Meantone (Renaissance/Baroque)

Arabic Maqamat - Rast, Bayati, Saba, Hijaz, Nahawand, Kurd

Indian Classical - Hindustani: Bhairav, Yaman, Todi, Bhairavi - Carnatic: Shankarabharana, Mayamalavagowla

East Asian - Chinese Pentatonic - Japanese Hirajoshi

Indonesian Gamelan - Pelog (7-note with characteristic unequal intervals) - Slendro (5-note with roughly equal ~240-cent steps)

Each scale generates its own set of colored lines, with the spacing reflecting the actual intervals of that tuning system. When you select Maqam Hijaz, for instance, you’ll see the characteristic augmented second interval as a visibly larger gap between scale lines.

Modulations: When the Staff Transforms

One of the most striking visual features of the Score Canvas is what happens during a scale modulation. When the active scale changes at a specific point in time, the staff lines don’t simply switch—they physically shift to reflect the new intervallic relationships.

Imagine you’re composing a piece that begins in Just Intonation and modulates to Maqam Hijaz. At the modulation point, the evenly-spaced lines of Just Intonation suddenly rearrange: some intervals compress, others expand dramatically (like the characteristic augmented second of Hijaz). The visual effect is immediate and unmistakable—you can see the harmonic world changing.

This applies to any scale change: moving from a 7-note Western scale to a 5-note pentatonic visibly reduces the density of lines. Switching from equal temperament to Pythagorean tuning subtly shifts the spacing of each interval. The canvas becomes a living map of the harmonic terrain, reshaping itself as the music demands.

Two Modes: Musical and Absolute

The Score Canvas offers two fundamentally different ways of working:

Musical Mode

In Musical mode, the canvas operates within a metrical framework: - Bar lines divide time into measures - Tempo controls the relationship between visual distance and musical time - Time signatures define the metric grouping

Time signature changes are visually striking—when the meter shifts from 4/4 to 7/8, the bar line spacing changes accordingly, making rhythmic structure immediately apparent.

Arbitrary Time Signatures: Beyond the Conventional

Because Kala represents time in milliseconds underneath the musical abstraction, time signatures aren’t limited to conventional fractions. You can define a measure as 13/43 or 51/97 or any ratio you can imagine. The system simply calculates the duration in milliseconds and spaces the bar lines accordingly.

This opens genuinely new rhythmic territory. Traditional notation systems are practically limited to denominators that relate to standard note values (4, 8, 16, etc.) and numerators that performers can count. Kala has no such constraint. Want a measure that’s exactly 1.7 times a quarter note? Define it. Want to explore prime-number relationships between measures? Go ahead. The mathematics of rhythm become as fluid as the mathematics of pitch.

This doesn’t mean conventional time signatures are deprecated—4/4 and 3/4 work exactly as expected. But the door is open to metric structures that traditional notation couldn’t practically represent, just as the scale system opens doors to tuning structures that keyboard instruments couldn’t play.

This mode is ideal for composers working within traditional metric frameworks, preparing scores that need to communicate with performers, or exploring entirely new rhythmic territories that only become practical when time is fundamentally continuous.

Absolute Mode

In Absolute mode, the canvas becomes a pure time-frequency field with no imposed boundaries. There are no bar lines, no metric grid—just continuous time flowing from left to right. This mode is suited for: - Electroacoustic composition where traditional meter doesn’t apply - Sound design and texture creation - Free-form exploration without metric constraints - Music that treats time as a continuum rather than a grid

You can switch between modes as needed, or use Musical mode with unconventional time signatures to create structured complexity.

Drawing Notes

Pen or Mouse

Kala works naturally with both mouse and pen tablet. The basic mechanics are the same in either case:

  • Horizontal motion defines time (left to right)
  • Vertical position defines pitch

When using a pressure-sensitive pen tablet, pen pressure is also sampled continuously throughout the stroke and stored as the note's initial dynamics curve. A light touch creates a soft note; pressing harder creates a louder one. You can crescendo by increasing pressure as you draw, or produce a sforzando attack by pressing hard at first and then easing off.

When drawing with a mouse, the dynamics curve starts flat. You can then shape it precisely using control points on the note blob or the curve dialog.

Discrete Notes: Stepped Pitch

In Discrete mode, notes snap to the nearest scale line. This is similar to traditional notation—each note has a single, definite pitch. You draw the horizontal extent of the note, and Kala places it on the closest scale degree.

These notes appear as colored “blobs” on the canvas. Each note takes its color from the track it belongs to, allowing you to instantly identify which instrument or voice a note is assigned to, even when multiple tracks overlap in the same pitch range.

Continuous Notes: Drawn Pitch Curves

In Continuous mode, your vertical motion during drawing is captured as a pitch curve. This allows for:

  • Glissando: Smooth slides between pitches
  • Portamento: Gradual pitch transitions
  • Microtonal inflections: Pitch bends and ornaments
  • Unconventional melodies: Pitches that don’t align with any scale

The resulting note displays its pitch curve as the center line of the blob, showing exactly how the pitch evolves over time. You can later “quantize” a continuous note to the scale, converting the smooth glissando into stepped transitions between scale degrees—useful for exploring a melodic idea freely and then locking it to a tuning system.

The Note Blob: Visualizing Expression

Anatomy of a Blob

Each note appears as an organic shape rather than a geometric symbol. This shape encodes multiple dimensions of expression:

The Center Line traces the pitch over time. For discrete notes, it’s a horizontal line at the note’s frequency. For continuous notes, it curves to show the pitch contour.

The Top Edge represents dynamics (amplitude). Where you pressed harder, the blob is taller; where you pressed lighter, it’s shorter. This creates an instant visual representation of the note’s loudness envelope.

The Bottom Edge displays vibrato intensity when vibrato is active. As the vibrato develops (based on its onset time), the bottom edge expands to show the increasing oscillation amplitude.

The Color indicates which track the note belongs to. Each track has its own color, making it easy to distinguish between different instruments or voices even when they share the same pitch range. The scale lines provide harmonic context; the note colors provide orchestration context.

What You See Is What You Hear

The blob visualization creates a direct correspondence between visual appearance and sonic result:

  • Tall sections are loud; short sections are soft
  • Wide blobs are long notes; narrow blobs are short notes
  • Curved center lines mean pitch is changing
  • Wavy bottom edges indicate vibrato
  • Color shows which track/instrument the note belongs to
  • Position relative to colored scale lines shows harmonic function

This makes the score readable at a glance—you can see the shape of a phrase, identify dynamic swells, spot pitch inflections, and understand the orchestration without decoding abstract symbols.

The Curve System: Composing Expression

Curves as a Compositional Language

Every note in Kala can carry any number of named curves — continuous envelopes that evolve over the note’s duration. A curve is a shape in time: it can be a simple ramp, a multi-segment envelope with smooth or stepped transitions, or any custom contour you design. The power of the system lies in routing: each curve can be connected to any modulatable parameter in the Sounit Builder, turning it into a live control signal that drives the synthesis engine sample by sample during playback.

The Pitch Curve

Every note has a built-in pitch curve representing fundamental frequency over time. For discrete notes it is constant (a single scale degree). For continuous notes it follows the vertical path you drew, or can be edited point-by-point after the fact.

The Dynamics Curve

Every note also has a built-in dynamics curve controlling amplitude over time. It can originate in three ways:

  • Pen pressure — when drawing with a pen tablet, pressure is sampled continuously and stored as the dynamics curve. The shape of your stroke becomes the loudness envelope of the note.
  • Control-point editing — drag the dots along the top edge of a selected note blob to sculpt the dynamics curve by hand directly on the canvas.
  • Apply Dynamics Curve dialog — right-click a note and choose Apply Dynamics Curve… to open a full envelope editor. Choose from presets (Crescendo, Swell, Fade Out, and more) or draw a custom shape, then apply it at the weight you choose.

User-Defined Curves

Beyond dynamics, each note can hold any number of additional user-defined curves. These have no intrinsic meaning — their effect is determined entirely by how they are wired in the Sounit Builder.

You give each curve a name — for instance brightness, breathiness, or resonance — and design its shape in the curve editor. In the Sounit Builder, any modulatable input port can accept a named note curve as its source. When the note plays, each curve becomes a real-time modulation signal for its assigned parameter.

Several curves can run simultaneously on the same note. For example:

  • A dynamics curve fades the note in slowly over its first half
  • A brightness curve opens a high-frequency rolloff during the same span
  • A body curve swells a formant resonance near the peak and lets it decay

Each curve is routed independently, and the number of curves per note is unlimited. The result is a general-purpose language for shaping any dimension of the sound across time — from broad dynamic arcs to the subtlest timbral evolution — written directly into the score.

Unique names per track: Every named curve must have a unique name within the track. If you try to add a curve with a name that already exists somewhere in the track, Kala will warn you and ask you to choose a different name. This uniqueness rule is what makes the Apply feature possible — a name unambiguously identifies a curve shape across all notes.

Editing Curves

After drawing a note, curves can be refined in several ways:

  • Top-edge control points — when a note is selected, drag the dots along its top edge to adjust the active curve directly on the canvas. Changes are immediate.
  • Apply Dynamics Curve dialog — right-click → Apply Dynamics Curve… to use presets or draw an arbitrary dynamics shape.
  • Apply Expressive Curve dialog — right-click → Apply Expressive Curve… to progressively shape a named expressive curve across the selection. A dropdown lets you choose which named curve to target; the envelope shape you design is applied as a multiplier across the phrase span.
  • Expressive Curves inspector — the inspector panel in the Score tab provides full control over all curves on the selected note or selection: add new curves, delete existing ones, or apply a previously defined curve shape to new notes.

The curve editing is non-destructive — you can always return and reshape a curve after it has been applied.

The Expressive Curves Inspector

Select one or more notes to reveal the Expressive Curves panel in the Note inspector. It contains a dropdown and three buttons.

The Dropdown

Lists every curve carried by the first selected note: Dynamics is always present at the top; any additional named curves follow. The dropdown serves two purposes:

  • Canvas display selector — whichever curve is chosen becomes the one drawn on the canvas. Notes that carry that named curve display it; notes that do not fall back to showing their Dynamics curve. At any given moment only two curve shapes are visible at once: the chosen curve (on notes that have it) and Dynamics (on the rest).
  • Editing target — the top-edge control dots on selected notes always edit whichever curve the dropdown is showing. Switch the dropdown to switch which curve you are sculpting.

Automatic reset on selection change: If you click on a note that does not carry the currently displayed curve, the dropdown and canvas automatically reset to Dynamics. This keeps the display consistent — you never end up with the dropdown showing a curve that the selected note cannot display. From the Dynamics baseline you can then choose any curve you want.

Add

Opens a two-step dialog:

  1. Name the curve — type a unique name for the new curve. The name must not already exist anywhere in the track. If it does, Kala will warn you and re-open the name prompt so you can choose a different one.
  2. Design the shape — the Dynamics Curve dialog opens so you can choose a preset envelope (Crescendo, Swell, Fade Out, etc.) or draw a completely custom shape. Set the weight (how strongly the curve modulates its target parameter) and whether the shape should be applied per note or stretched across the entire selection.

The new curve is added to every note in the current selection and immediately becomes the active curve in the dropdown.

Delete

Removes the currently selected curve from the note. Only available when a named curve (not Dynamics) is selected and exactly one note is selected. Dynamics cannot be deleted.

Apply

Reuses a curve shape that already exists somewhere in the track and copies it to the current selection. Clicking Apply opens a small dialog listing all named curves found across every note in the track. Choose one and click OK — that curve's shape is written to all selected notes:

  • Notes that already carry a curve with that name have it replaced.
  • Notes that do not yet carry that curve have it added.

The Apply button is disabled (greyed out) when no named curves exist anywhere in the track yet.

Apply is fully undoable.

Vibrato: A Parameter, Not an Ornament

Traditional notation indicates vibrato with a wavy line or leaves it to performer interpretation. In Kala, vibrato is a structured parameter with its own envelope:

Onset: How far into the note before vibrato begins (0.0-1.0) Rate: Oscillation frequency in Hz Pitch Depth: How far the pitch deviates (in cents or Hz) Amplitude Depth: How much the loudness oscillates Envelope: How the vibrato intensity develops (attack, hold, decay)

This allows for historically-informed vibrato (Baroque style with late onset and subtle depth), operatic vibrato (immediate onset with wide pitch deviation), or any custom style. The vibrato envelope is visualized on the note’s bottom edge, showing exactly when and how intensely the vibrato develops.

Segments: Subdividing Continuous Notes

When you quantize a continuous note to the scale, Kala creates segments—discrete subdivisions where each section has a constant pitch. This is useful for:

  • Converting a freely-drawn glissando into stepped scale degrees
  • Assigning different sound variations to different parts of a note
  • Creating complex melodic ornaments within a single gesture

Each segment can have its own: - Pitch (snapped to a scale degree) - Sound variation (different timbre from the same instrument) - Vibrato settings

Double-clicking a quantized note enters segment editing mode, where you can adjust individual segments, change their pitches, or delete sections.

Legato: Connected Notes

Notes can be marked as legato, indicating a smooth connection from the previous note without re-articulation. Visually, legato notes display with half the intensity of the track's color.

In the sound engine, legato affects how envelopes behave—attack phases are skipped, creating seamless transitions rather than discrete re-attacks. This is essential for vocal lines, bowed strings, and wind instruments where breath or bow continues across note boundaries.

Time and Navigation

The Timeline

The horizontal axis represents time. In Absolute mode, time is measured in milliseconds—a pure continuum. In Musical mode, time is organized by tempo and meter, with bar lines marking measure boundaries.

A timeline at the top of the canvas shows: - Current playback position (the “now” marker) - Loop region boundaries - Scale modulation points (where the active scale changes) - Bar lines and time signature markers (in Musical mode)

When time signatures change in Musical mode, the bar line spacing adjusts immediately—switching from 4/4 to 6/8 visibly compresses the distance between bar lines, while moving to 5/4 expands them. Combined with scale modulations that reshape the vertical spacing, the canvas provides an instant visual map of both metric and harmonic structure.

Zoom and Pan

The canvas supports independent zoom on both axes:

  • Horizontal zoom (time axis): Ctrl+= to zoom in, Ctrl+− to zoom out, Ctrl+0 to fit all content in view. Alternatively press Z to activate zoom mode and drag the mouse (up/left = zoom in, down/right = zoom out), or use the mouse wheel while in zoom mode.
  • Vertical zoom (frequency axis): available via the zoom toolbar controls.
  • Pan: press Ctrl+Space to toggle pan mode, then drag anywhere to shift the view. The scroll bars also work at all times.

The frequency range spans roughly 20 Hz to 8000 Hz by default. Press G to open the Go To dialog and jump directly to a specific time or bar number without scrolling manually.

Canvas Interactions

Mouse

The canvas responds differently depending on the active tool.

In Draw modes (D or C): left-click and drag horizontally to draw a note. Vertical position at the start of the drag determines pitch (snapping to the nearest scale line in Discrete mode). In Continuous mode your vertical motion throughout the drag is captured as the pitch curve.

In Select mode (S): left-click a note to select it; left-click empty canvas and drag to rubber-band select all notes that fall within the rectangle. Ctrl+click toggles a note in or out of the current selection. Left-click and drag on a selected note to move the entire selection. The resize handles at the left and right edges of a note let you adjust its start time and duration independently. Drag the top edge control points to edit the dynamics curve.

The mouse wheel scrolls vertically through pitch in all modes.

Right-Click: Note Context Menu

Right-clicking on a selection of one or more notes opens a context menu. Some options are only meaningful with multiple notes and are grayed out when a single note is selected (marked 2+ notes below).

Item What it does
Quantize to Scale Snaps the pitch of each selected note to the nearest scale degree. For continuous notes, the entire pitch curve is quantized point by point, converting the smooth glide into stepped scale transitions.
Make Continuous Converts discrete notes to continuous notes, freeing their pitch from scale-snapping so the pitch curve can be drawn and edited freely.
Make Discrete Converts continuous notes back to discrete notes, snapping each to the nearest scale degree and discarding the pitch curve.
Apply Variation → Assigns a sound variation from the current track's sounit to the selected notes. Base Sounit removes any variation assignment. This lets different notes use different timbres from the same instrument graph—useful for articulation changes, register differences, or timbral evolution across a phrase.
Easing… (2+ notes) Applies rhythmic easing to the timing of the selected notes, spacing them using an easing curve (ease-in, ease-out, etc.) rather than at their original intervals. Useful for accelerandos, rallentandos, and expressive timing shapes.
Apply Dynamics Curve… Opens a dialog for shaping the dynamics (amplitude) of the selected notes using a curve you design or choose from presets. Works on a single note or any number of notes.

Curve editor — Click to add control points, drag to move them, right-click or drag off-canvas to delete. Ctrl-click a point to cycle its segment type: linear (straight ramp), smooth (cosine ease, green dot), or step (hold then jump, orange dot). Presets cover common shapes (Crescendo, Decrescendo, Swell, Fade Out, etc.) and custom curves can be saved and loaded as .env.json files.

Weight — Scales how strongly the curve is applied. At 0 nothing changes; at 1 the full effect is applied; above 1 the effect is exaggerated.

Per note button — Controls how the curve is mapped:
  • Off (selection mode) — The curve spans the entire selection from first note start to last note end. Each note receives a single amplitude multiplier determined by where its center falls on the curve. Use this to shape a phrase as a whole—crescendo across ten notes, for example.
  • On (per-note mode) — The curve is applied independently to each note, mapping its full shape across that note's own duration. Every note in the selection gets the same curve shape. Use this to give individual notes an internal dynamic contour—a swell, a fade, or any envelope—regardless of their position in the selection.
Apply Expressive Curve… Progressively shapes a named expressive curve across the selected notes using an envelope you design or choose from presets. Available only when the selection contains at least one note that already carries a named curve (added via the Expressive Curves inspector).

Target curve dropdown — Lists every distinct named curve present across the selected notes. Choose the one you want to modulate. Notes that do not carry the chosen curve are left untouched.

Curve editor — Same editor as Apply Dynamics Curve: click to add points, drag to move, right-click or drag off-canvas to delete. Ctrl-click a point to cycle its segment type (linear / smooth / step). All presets and Save / Load are available.

Weight — Scales the strength of the effect (0 = no change, 1 = full effect, >1 = exaggerated).

The curve always spans the whole selection (no per-note option). Each note receives a single scale factor determined by where its centre falls on the envelope. For example, a Crescendo shape applied to ten notes with a brightness curve will make the first note the least bright and the last note the most bright — exactly like a dynamic crescendo, but on any expressive dimension you choose.
Link as Legato (2+ notes) Connects the selected notes into a legato phrase. The sound engine skips the attack phase on every note except the first, creating smooth transitions without re-articulation.
Unlink (Staccato) Breaks a legato group back into individually articulated notes, restoring the attack phase on each.
Scale Timing… Proportionally scales the start times and durations of the selected notes by a factor you specify. Useful for fitting a phrase into a different time span or adjusting local tempo without changing the global tempo map.

Right-Click: Segment Context Menu

When segment editing mode is active (enter it by double-clicking a quantized continuous note), right-clicking a segment opens a different menu.

Item What it does
Change Pitch → Reassigns this segment to any scale degree. The submenu lists every pitch available on the current scale.

Timeline Interactions

The thin strip at the top of the canvas controls playback position and loop regions.

  • Left-click on the timeline to move the now marker (playback position) to that time.
  • L activates loop mode — then drag on the timeline to define the loop region start and end.
  • Delete clears the loop region when no notes are selected.

From Score to Sound

The Score Canvas is the compositional side of Kala — where musical ideas are captured as notes and shaped through curves. Each note stores its pitch over time, its dynamics curve, its vibrato parameters, its segment divisions, and any number of user-defined curves that target specific parameters in the synthesis engine.

When you play back the composition, the sound engine reads all of these curves sample-by-sample. There is no quantization to MIDI values, no velocity layers approximating dynamics. The continuous curves in the score become continuous modulation of the synthesis parameters — amplitude, spectral character, resonance, and anything else you have wired up — with complete fidelity.

This is the core philosophy of Kala: expression is written into the composition itself. What the score says is exactly what you hear.

A Different Kind of Literacy

Learning to read and write in the Score Canvas requires developing new intuitions. Instead of memorizing note positions on a fixed staff, you learn to read pitch from position relative to the colored scale lines—the red line is always the tonic, the blue line is always the fifth, regardless of which scale you’re using. Instead of interpreting dynamic markings (pp, mf, ff), you see the actual amplitude envelope as the shape of the note.

For composers coming from traditional notation, this can feel disorienting at first—where are the key signatures? But then you realize: the scale lines are the key signature, made visible and dynamic. When you modulate, the staff itself transforms, making the harmonic shift impossible to miss. Time signatures exist in Musical mode, with bar lines that visually reshape when the meter changes.

For those new to music notation entirely, the Score Canvas may actually be more intuitive than traditional systems. The visual representation maps directly to sonic reality: higher is higher, louder is taller, longer is wider. Modulations literally change the landscape. There’s no symbolic indirection to decode.

Summary

The Score Canvas reimagines musical notation for the digital age:

  • Frequency-based staff with color-coded scale lines that adapt to any tuning system
  • Dynamic modulation where scale changes visibly reshape the staff spacing
  • Two modes: Musical (with bar lines, tempo, time signatures) and Absolute (pure time-frequency field)
  • Pen pressure capture that records dynamics as a continuous curve as you draw
  • Unlimited user-defined curves per note, each routable to any modulatable parameter in the synthesis engine
  • Blob visualization that shows pitch, dynamics, and vibrato as organic shapes
  • Track-based note colors for clear orchestration visibility
  • Non-destructive curve editing via on-canvas control points, presets, or the full curve designer
  • World scales supporting Western, Arabic, Indian, and Asian musical traditions
  • Continuous pitch for glissando, portamento, and microtonal expression
  • Segment system for subdividing notes with independent parameters

This is not notation as instruction — it’s notation as composed performance, where every dimension of the sound is shaped through curves that live inside the score and drive the synthesis engine directly.
